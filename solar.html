Building Something Different

I wanted to see what an alternative might look like as a live experiment.

You're reading this article on a solar-powered server built on a ¬£25 Raspberry Pi, powered by a ¬£50 solar panel, and backed by a ¬£25 battery. It should be able to hold 64 tracks and support up to 25 listeners. When the sun shines, it works. When it doesn't, it doesn't.

Of course, the setup itself carries a footprint: the embodied energy in the panel, battery, and transformer is real. But unlike per-stream energy use, these are largely one-off costs. More importantly, the model could scale‚Äîsolar-powered infrastructure could support larger farms, shared networks, or even parts of the traditional streaming stack.

Meanwhile, Spotify‚Äîdespite massive scale‚Äîoperates at a loss and continues to draw heavy capital investment. If even a fraction of that investment were redirected into distributed solar infrastructure, we could begin to shrink streaming's hidden footprint.

Context & Inspiration

This project stands on the shoulders of others who've been exploring solar-powered digital infrastructure for years. Low-Tech Magazine runs their entire website on solar power‚Äîit goes dark when it's cloudy in Barcelona, turning constraints into editorial policy. Solar Protocol hosts a network of decentralised, solar-powered web servers that shift traffic based on which location has the most sun.

Both projects provided crucial inspiration and practical guidance. Low-Tech Magazine's detailed documentation of their solar setup showed me what was possible with minimal hardware, while Solar Protocol's open-source approach demonstrated how to build resilient, weather-dependent systems.

I'm indebted to their pioneering work‚Äîwithout their guides, tutorials, and proof that solar web infrastructure actually works, I couldn't have built radtypo. They showed that embracing energy constraints isn't a limitation‚Äîit's a design philosophy.

What I Learned

Building radtypo taught me things I hadn't expected. Streaming platforms optimize for discovery and infinite choice, but that creates a kind of attention scarcity‚Äîmost listeners skip songs within 30 seconds. When your server only holds 64 tracks, listening becomes more intentional. You're more likely to hear a song through to the end.

The solar constraint was equally revealing. When the server goes offline at dusk, you realize how much we've normalized instant availability. There's something meditative about waiting for the sun to return, about music that has seasons and moods tied to actual weather.

Hand-encoding each track, writing metadata, crafting pages‚Äîthis forced a different relationship with the music. Every song had to earn its place. In a world of algorithmic curation, manual curation felt radical.

Why This Matters

This isn't about converting everyone to solar streaming. Most people just want music that works when they press play‚Äîand that's completely valid.

But rad.typo asks different questions: What is our digital convenience actually costing? What happens when we build tools that admit their limitations instead of hiding them? Could we find meaning in constraint rather than always seeking to eliminate it?

Maybe we don't need infinite music libraries. Maybe we need better relationships with the music we already have.

rad.typo is a quiet refusal in physical form‚Äînot a solution, but an invitation to consider alternatives.
